What is Socialization?

Definition: The lifelong process where individuals learn attitudes, values, and behaviors appropriate for their society.

Identity: Our self-concept is shaped through socialization‚Äîhow we see ourselves and how others see us.

Nature vs. Nurture Debate:

  • Nature: Traits we are born with (heredity).
  • Nurture: Traits acquired through experiences (environment).

Case Studies on Isolation & Importance of Socialization

Isabelle (1938):

  • Lived in isolation for 6 years in a dark room with no social contact.
  • Initially behaved like an infant but later developed language skills and improved socially.

Genie (1970s):

  • Kept in isolation from 20 months to 14 years.
  • After rescue, she never fully developed language abilities, proving that socialization is crucial in early development.

Primate Studies (Harry Harlow, 1971)

Harlow's rhesus monkey experiment showed that:

  • Monkeys raised in isolation became fearful, withdrawn, and abusive parents.
  • Monkeys preferred soft cloth-covered surrogates over wire ones that provided food, proving that affection is more important than basic survival needs.

Heredity & Identical Twins

Case of Oskar Stohr & Jack Yufe:

  • One was a Nazi supporter, the other worked in the Israeli army.
  • Despite different upbringings, they shared similar habits, voice patterns, and intelligence, showing that both genes and environment shape identity.

üß† Theories of the Self & Socialization

1. Charles Cooley ‚Äì "Looking-Glass Self"

  • Our self-image is based on how we believe others see us.
  • Example: If a teacher criticizes you, you may think "I am stupid", even if it's not true.

2. George Herbert Mead ‚Äì Stages of the Self

  • Preparatory Stage (Infancy): Imitating others.
  • Play Stage (Toddlerhood): Role-taking through pretend play (e.g., pretending to be a teacher).
  • Game Stage (Ages 8-9): Understanding multiple roles in society (e.g., teamwork in baseball).
  • Generalized Other (Ages 10+): Considering how society as a whole views actions (e.g., "What would society think if I cheat on a test?").

3. Erving Goffman ‚Äì Dramaturgical Approach

  • We act like performers in a play, changing our behavior based on social settings.
  • Face-work: Efforts to avoid embarrassment (e.g., pretending to be busy when a boss walks by).

4. Sigmund Freud ‚Äì Psychological Theory of the Self

  • The self has opposing forces: Biological urges (pleasure-seeking) vs. Society's rules (self-control).

üìå Life Course & Socialization Stages

1. Rites of Passage

Ceremonies that mark life transitions (e.g., circumcision in Turkey, retirement celebrations).

2. Retirement Stages

  1. Honeymoon phase
  2. Disenchantment (boredom/depression)
  3. Reorientation (adjusting to a new routine)

3. Anticipatory Socialization & Resocialization

  • Anticipatory Socialization: Practicing future roles (e.g., job internships).
  • Resocialization: Learning new behaviors (e.g., military training).

Total Institutions (Goffman, 1961): Places where all aspects of life are controlled (e.g., prisons, monasteries).

üìå Agents of Socialization

1. Family

  • Primary agent of socialization‚Äîteaches language, cultural values, and gender roles.
  • Impact of race & gender: Media often reinforces negative stereotypes (e.g., lack of Black representation in children's books).

2. Schools

  • Formal socialization (academic learning).
  • Hidden curriculum: Teaches discipline, respect, and societal norms.
  • Conflict Theory View: Schools reinforce inequality (e.g., education is expensive).

3. Peer Groups

Influence behavior and social identity, especially in teenage years.

4. Mass Media

TV, social media, and the internet shape attitudes and opinions.

5. Workplace

  • Resocialization occurs when adapting to new jobs.
  • People change jobs 11 times between ages 18-40.
