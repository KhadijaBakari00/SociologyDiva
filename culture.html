Culture

The fabric of human society ✨

📌 Key Concepts

1. What is Culture?

Definition: Culture is the totality of learned, socially transmitted customs, knowledge, material objects, and behavior.

"Culture is that complex whole which includes knowledge, belief, art, morals, law, customs, and any other capabilities and habits acquired by man as a member of society." — E.B. Tylor

Culture vs. Society:

  • Culture = Shared customs, values, and material objects
  • Society = A group of people who share a culture and live in the same territory

2. Cultural Universals (George Murdock)

Cultural Universals: Common cultural elements found in all societies (e.g., marriage, language, religious rituals, cooking, personal names).

However, these elements vary in expression.

Example: Marriage exists in all societies, but in some, parents arrange it, while in others, individuals choose their partners.

3. Ethnocentrism & Cultural Relativism

  • Ethnocentrism: The belief that one's own culture is superior.
    • Example: Westerners judging Hindu culture for considering cows sacred.
  • Cultural Relativism: Understanding a culture from its own perspective rather than judging it.

4. Cultural Variation & Subcultures

  • Subculture: A distinct cultural group within a larger society that shares unique customs and traditions.
  • Argot: A specialized language/slang used by a subculture.
    • Example: Gamers, doctors, and hackers each have their own argot.

5. The Role of Language in Culture

  • Language is the foundation of every culture.
  • Cultural Capital: The knowledge and education a person has, which affects their status in society.
    • Example: Knowing multiple languages or classical art can increase cultural capital.
  • Language shapes reality:
    • Example: The Sami people (Northern Europe) have many words for snow, meaning they perceive it in ways others do not.

6. Symbols & Nonverbal Communication

  • Symbols: Objects, gestures, or words with meaning.
    • Example: A gold star sticker represents success.
  • Nonverbal Communication:
    • Facial expressions, body language, and gestures vary by culture.

7. Key Elements of Culture

  • Beliefs – What people assume to be true (e.g., belief in luck, spirits, or democracy)
  • Values – Social agreements on what is right and wrong
  • Norms – Rules of behavior
    • Folkways: Traditional customs (e.g., saying "excuse me" after sneezing)
    • Laws: Legal norms (e.g., stopping at a red light)
    • Mores: Morally significant norms (e.g., prohibition of child abuse)
  • Sanctions – Rewards and punishments for behavior

8. Cultural Change & Technology

Technology impacts culture:

  • Industrialization changed how people work, interact, and view social roles
  • Example: The internet transformed communication and social relationships

9. Types of Societies Based on Technology

  1. Hunting & Gathering Societies – Small, nomadic groups relying on nature
  2. Horticultural & Pastoral Societies – Early farming and domestication of animals
  3. Agrarian Societies – Large-scale farming, leading to social inequality
  4. Industrial Societies – Factories, urbanization, and major technological advancements
  5. Post-Industrial Societies – Economy based on information and services (e.g., digital age)

10. Gemeinschaft vs. Gesellschaft (Ferdinand Tönnies)

  • Gemeinschaft (Community): Close-knit, traditional, personal relationships
    • Example: Small villages where everyone knows each other
  • Gesellschaft (Modern Society): Impersonal, professional relationships
    • Example: A city where people interact for business rather than personal connections

📖 Famous Quotes

"Culture is the learned behavior that distinguishes one group from another." — E.B. Tylor
"Cultural universals exist, but how they are expressed varies." — George Murdock
"We do not see things as they are, we see them as we are." — Cultural Relativism Concept
"Language does more than describe reality; it shapes it." — Linguistic Determinism
"Technology changes, but culture evolves with it." — Sociocultural Evolution

B) A speculative statement about relationships between variables

C) A theory about socialization
