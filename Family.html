Family

The foundation of society ✨

📌 Key Definitions

What is Family?

Narrow definition: People linked by kinship (blood, marriage, adoption).

Broad definition: Group sharing responsibility for caregiving and reproduction.

Household ≠ Family: (e.g., roommates can share a household but aren't family).

"The family is the nucleus of civilization." – Will Durant

📌 Types of Family

Nuclear Family

Parents + children in one household (traditional Western model).

Extended Family

Includes relatives like aunts, grandparents, etc. (common in many cultures).

Reconstituted Family

Stepfamilies, remarriages with children from previous unions.

Single-Parent Family

One parent raising children (increasingly common).

Childless Family

Couples without children by choice or circumstance.

📌 Forms of Marriage

Monogamy

One spouse at a time (most common globally).

Polygyny

One man, multiple wives (found in some African and Middle Eastern cultures).

Polyandry

One woman, multiple husbands (e.g., Nyinba in Nepal).

Serial Monogamy

Multiple marriages but only one spouse at a time (common in Western societies).

📌 Kinship Patterns

Bilateral

Both sides of the family are equally important (common in Western societies).

Patrilineal

Father's side dominant (common in many traditional societies).

Matrilineal

Mother's side dominant (e.g., some Native American tribes).

📌 Authority Patterns

Patriarchy

Men dominate family decision-making (historically common).

Matriarchy

Women dominate (rare, but found in some societies).

Egalitarian

Equal power between partners (increasingly common).

📌 Mate Selection

Endogamy

Marrying within group (e.g., same religion, caste).

Exogamy

Marrying outside group (e.g., no incest).

Homogamy

Marrying someone with similar traits (class, race, education).

📌 Functions of Family (Functionalist View)

From William Ogburn:

  1. Reproduction
  2. Protection
  3. Socialization
  4. Regulation of sexual behavior
  5. Affection & companionship
  6. Provision of social status

📌 Sociological Perspectives on Family

Conflict Theory

Families perpetuate inequality and patriarchy.

Feminist View

Challenges gender roles, examines domestic labor division.

Interactionist View

Focuses on relationships within families (e.g., involved fathers reduce delinquency).

📌 Childhood & Ariès

Philippe Ariès: Childhood is a modern concept; children were once treated like mini-adults.

  • Medieval children were apprenticed early.
  • Shift in 18th-20th century to emotional, child-centered families.

📌 Modern Family Trends

Boomerang Generation

Adult children returning to live with parents.

Sandwich Generation

Adults caring for both children and aging parents.

Delayed Marriage

People marrying later in life.

Rise in Single-Parent Families

Due to divorce, choice, or widowhood.
