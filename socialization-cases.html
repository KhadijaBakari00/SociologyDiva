üìå Summary: Key Concepts

What is Socialization?

The lifelong process where individuals learn values, norms, behaviors, and social skills necessary for society.

Key Cases of Social Isolation

Isabelle (1938):

  • Isolated for first 6 years with only her deaf-mute mother
  • Initially behaved like a wild animal (scored as infant on tests)
  • With therapy, reached 6th-grade level by age 14
  • Takeaway: Shows potential for recovery with intensive socialization

Genie (1970s):

  • Locked in room from 20 months to age 14
  • Never developed full language despite therapy
  • Takeaway: Critical period for language development exists

Romanian Orphanages (1990s):

  • Children left in cribs 18-20 hours/day with minimal care
  • Showed fear of human contact and antisocial behavior
  • Takeaway: Emotional neglect causes lasting damage

Nature vs. Nurture Debate

Modern consensus: Both genetics (nature) and environment (nurture) interact to shape development.

Twin Studies: Oskar & Jack

  • Identical twins separated at birth
  • Oskar raised in Nazi Germany, Jack by Jewish father in Trinidad
  • Shared mannerisms (storing rubber bands, flushing toilets before use)
  • Different political views and cultural habits
  • Takeaway: Genetics influence temperament, but socialization shapes values

üß† Theories of the Self

Cooley ‚Äì Looking-Glass Self

We develop self-image through 3 steps:

  1. Imagine how we appear to others
  2. Imagine their judgment of us
  3. Develop feelings (pride/shame) based on perceived judgment

Even incorrect perceptions shape identity!

Mead ‚Äì Stages of the Self

  • Preparatory Stage: Imitation without understanding (infants)
  • Play Stage: Role-taking through pretend play (toddlers)
  • Game Stage: Understands multiple social roles (age 8-9)
