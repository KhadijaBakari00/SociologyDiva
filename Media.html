üìå History of Media Communication

Media and social change: From hunter-gatherer societies to industrial ones, media has influenced human interaction.

Media Evolution:

  1. Age of Signs: Nonverbal gestures/sounds (e.g., vervet monkeys)
  2. Age of Speech: Oral communication
  3. Age of Writing: 5,000 years ago
  4. Age of Print: Gutenberg press (1455)
  5. Age of Electronics: Music, film, television
  6. Information Age: Internet, digital media, mobile phones
"The medium is the message." - Marshall McLuhan

üìå What are Mass Media?

Mass media = technologies + organizations for large-scale, impersonal communication (e.g., TV, radio, social media, newspapers).

Functions:

  • Information dissemination
  • Entertainment
  • Socialization
  • Promotion of consumption
  • Surveillance of environment

üìå Optimistic vs. Pessimistic Views

Optimistic View:

  • Increases choice and access to information
  • Enables participation (e.g., citizen journalism)
  • Facilitates global connections
  • Empowers marginalized voices

Pessimistic View:

  • Causes social isolation
  • Spreads misinformation
  • Lacks regulation
  • Promotes consumerism
  • Creates digital divides

üìå Media as an Agent of Socialization

  • Secondary agent‚Äîshapes identity, norms, behavior
  • Children watch 6+ hours of media a day (UK data)
  • Influences attitudes, trends, and knowledge
  • Can reinforce or challenge stereotypes

üß† Sociological Perspectives on Media

Functionalist View

Functions:

  • Agent of socialization
  • Enforcer of social norms
  • Conferral of status
  • Promotion of consumption

Dysfunction: Narcotizing effect‚Äîinformation overload leads to apathy.

Conflict View

Media reflects and reinforces power structures.

Key concepts:

  • Gatekeeping: Control over what gets published
  • Media Monitoring: Surveillance without consent
  • Digital Divide: Unequal access to technology
  • Dominant Ideology: Reinforces elite cultural values
  • Stereotyping: Overgeneralized media portrayals

Feminist View

  • Underrepresentation and misrepresentation of women
  • Gender roles reinforced (e.g., women as housewives, men as strong leaders)
  • Critiques sexualization and unrealistic body images
  • Examines portrayal of masculinity

Interactionist View

  • Micro-level analysis of media interactions
  • How people interpret and use media
  • Social networking and digital relationships
  • Media's role in daily social behavior

üéØ Media Theories

1. Harold Lasswell (1948)

"Who says what, in which channel, to whom, with what effect?"

Focus: communication process and its impact.

2. Hypodermic Syringe Model

  • Media is "injected" into passive audiences
  • Messages received directly and uniformly
  • Assumes powerful media effects

3. Audience Reception Theory

  • Not all audiences react the same way
  • People interpret, dismiss, or resist messages
  • Active rather than passive audiences

4. Moral Panic ‚Äì Stanley Cohen

  • Media overreacts to perceived threats
  • Labels groups as "folk devils" (e.g., Mods & Rockers)
  • Results in stereotyping and exaggeration
  • Creates unnecessary public fear

5. Two-Step Flow Theory (Lazarsfeld)

Media ‚Üí Opinion Leaders ‚Üí General Public

6. Uses and Gratifications Theory

People actively choose media to fulfill needs (information, entertainment, etc.)

üìå Key Concepts

Media Convergence

Integration of media technologies (e.g., smartphones combining phone, camera, internet)

Global Village (McLuhan)

Media connects the world like a small community

Media Saturation

Overwhelming presence of media in daily life

Digital Divide

Inequality in access to digital technologies

Fake News

Deliberately misleading information presented as news
